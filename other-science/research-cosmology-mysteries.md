# Research: The Ten Biggest Mysteries in Cosmology

> Scope note: This document covers open problems specific to *cosmology* — the physics of the universe as a whole. Its sibling, `research-physics-mysteries.md`, covers foundational problems in physics generally. Several puzzles (the arrow of time, matter–antimatter asymmetry) have a foot in both; where that happens I flag the cross-reference rather than duplicate the analysis.

## 1. Question or Topic of Research

What are the ten deepest unsolved problems in cosmology as of 2026, and — crucially for fiction — what are the *actual candidate answers* researchers entertain, ranked by how seriously they're taken? I want the genuine epistemic status of each: which are near-crises with hard data behind them, which are elegant puzzles with no data at all, and which are popular online but softer than they look to working cosmologists.

## 2. Current Established Science (the ten, each with its candidate answers)

### 2.1 — Dark energy and the accelerating expansion
Since 1998 we've known the universe's expansion is *accelerating*, which requires either a repulsive energy component filling space (~68% of the cosmic energy budget) or a failure of general relativity on the largest scales. The default model, ΛCDM, treats it as a cosmological constant Λ — a fixed vacuum energy density. That default is now under real observational pressure. The DESI survey's second data release (March 2025) <cite index="28-1">raised the preference for a *time-evolving* dark-energy equation of state over a plain cosmological constant to 4.2σ (99.995%) when BAO data are combined with the CMB and supernovae</cite>, with independent support from the Dark Energy Survey and hints from ACT. **Candidate answers:** (a) a true cosmological constant, still viable but strained; (b) *quintessence* — a dynamical scalar field whose energy density evolves; (c) *phantom* or phantom-crossing dark energy, exotic and theoretically uncomfortable because it can violate energy conditions; (d) modified gravity; (e) unrecognized systematics in the supernova samples. **Bias flag:** the "evolving dark energy" signal is partly an artifact-risk zone. A pointed 2024 analysis argued the evidence is <cite index="30-1">strongly driven by the assumed parameter priors and the specific CPL parametrization, dubbing the effect the "PhantomX coincidence"</cite> — meaning the drama may live in the choice of fitting function rather than in the sky. Take the 4.2σ as real and exciting but *not* as settled discovery.

### 2.2 — The nature of dark matter
Roughly 27% of the universe is matter that gravitates but neither emits nor absorbs light. The evidence is overwhelming and multi-pronged (galaxy rotation curves, gravitational lensing, the CMB acoustic peaks, the Bullet Cluster, structure formation) — the *existence* of missing mass is about as solid as anything in cosmology. What it *is* remains completely unknown. **Candidate answers:** (a) WIMPs (weakly interacting massive particles) — the long-favored hypothesis, now in serious trouble as increasingly sensitive direct-detection experiments keep returning null results and steadily eat the theoretically motivated parameter space; (b) axions — light particles that would also solve the strong-CP problem (see physics doc §2.6), currently the most fashionable candidate; (c) sterile neutrinos; (d) primordial black holes; (e) MOND / modified gravity — which fits galaxy-scale data suspiciously well but fails at cluster and cosmological scales, so it can't be the whole story. Occasional indirect "hints" surface — e.g. a 2025 claim of <cite index="55-1">a gamma-ray halo signature from the galactic center matching predictions for WIMP annihilation</cite> — but these have a long history of not surviving scrutiny; treat any such announcement as unconfirmed until replicated.

### 2.3 — The Hubble tension
Two high-precision ways of measuring the universe's current expansion rate (H₀) disagree, and the gap is *widening as the data improve* — the opposite of what you'd expect if it were a mistake. The local distance-ladder method (Cepheids calibrating Type Ia supernovae) gives <cite index="52-1">H₀ = 73.0 ± 1.0 km/s/Mpc, while the CMB-calibrated ΛCDM inference from Planck gives 67.4 ± 0.5</cite> — now a >5σ discrepancy. The obvious escape hatch — that the local measurement is biased by crowded, blended Cepheid images — has been slammed shut: JWST's sharper resolution <cite index="48-1">confirmed the Hubble Space Telescope Cepheid photometry is not biased by crowding, ruling out that explanation at more than 8σ</cite>. **Candidate answers:** (a) *early dark energy* — a transient energy component near recombination that shrinks the sound horizon and reconciles the two; currently the most-studied new-physics fix; (b) other modifications to the early universe; (c) some *unknown* systematic common to one method; (d) evolving dark energy (ties to §2.1). No candidate is yet compelling. This is, alongside the cosmological constant problem, the most acute empirical crisis in the field — and unlike muon g-2, it has *strengthened* under scrutiny.

### 2.4 — Cosmic inflation: did it happen, and what drove it?
The leading account of the universe's first ~10⁻³² seconds posits a brief epoch of exponential expansion that flattens geometry, erases relics, and seeds structure from quantum fluctuations. Inflation is *hugely successful* at matching observations — the CMB's near-scale-invariant fluctuation spectrum is its signature prediction — but it is a framework, not a specific theory: there are hundreds of proposed inflaton potentials and no identification of the field itself. **Candidate answers / open sub-questions:** (a) which of the many inflaton models (if any) is correct — the "smoking gun" would be primordial gravitational waves imprinted as CMB B-mode polarization, so far undetected, which has already killed the simplest models; (b) whether inflation is *eternal*, spawning a multiverse of bubble universes — elegant, and possibly untestable, which makes some physicists regard it as metaphysics; (c) alternatives entirely, e.g. bouncing/cyclic cosmologies or string-gas scenarios. **Bias flag:** inflation's flexibility is both its strength and the standard critique against it — a framework that can accommodate almost any outcome risks being unfalsifiable.

### 2.5 — Baryon asymmetry: why is there something rather than nothing (in equal parts)?
The Big Bang should have produced matter and antimatter in equal amounts, which would have annihilated into radiation, leaving a universe of light and no atoms. Instead there's a tiny leftover of matter — roughly one part in a billion survived — and that residue is everything we see. The conditions required to generate such an asymmetry are known precisely (the *Sakharov conditions*: baryon-number violation, C and CP violation, departure from thermal equilibrium). The problem is that the Standard Model's known CP violation is *far* too small to produce the observed imbalance. **Candidate answers:** (a) *leptogenesis* — asymmetry generated first in the neutrino sector (via heavy Majorana neutrinos) and converted to baryons; currently the front-runner, and it ties directly to the neutrino-nature question in the physics doc (§2.5); (b) *electroweak baryogenesis* — requiring new physics at the electroweak scale; (c) GUT-scale baryogenesis. All require physics beyond the Standard Model. This is a genuine, hard, data-starved mystery.

### 2.6 — The cosmological constant problem (the vacuum catastrophe)
Distinct from §2.1 and arguably the single most embarrassing number in physics. Quantum field theory predicts the vacuum should carry an energy density from zero-point fluctuations. When you estimate it naively, the predicted value exceeds the observed dark-energy density by a factor of roughly **10¹²⁰** — the worst quantitative prediction in the history of science. Even with the most charitable cutoffs the discrepancy is dozens of orders of magnitude. **Candidate answers:** (a) an unknown symmetry or mechanism that cancels the vacuum energy almost — but not quite — perfectly (no such mechanism is known); (b) *anthropic / multiverse* selection — in a landscape of possible vacua, only those with a tiny Λ permit observers, so we necessarily find ourselves in one; deeply controversial, seen by critics as a surrender; (c) that our whole way of computing vacuum energy's gravitational effect is wrong. This is a "no data, pure crisis of theory" problem — the inverse of the Hubble tension.

### 2.7 — The low-entropy past (the cosmological arrow of time)
The second law of thermodynamics says entropy increases, which means the early universe must have been in an *extraordinarily* low-entropy state — far more special than mere randomness would ever produce. Why? The microscopic laws are (nearly) time-symmetric, yet the universe has a pronounced temporal direction, and that direction traces back to improbably ordered initial conditions. This is the cosmological face of the arrow-of-time problem (its foundational-physics face is in the physics doc §2.8). **Candidate answers:** (a) the *Past Hypothesis* — simply postulate the low-entropy start as a brute initial condition (unsatisfying to many); (b) inflation as an explanation for the special state (contested — critics argue it presupposes low entropy rather than explaining it); (c) cosmological models (Carroll–Chen, cyclic scenarios) that try to *derive* a low-entropy boundary from a larger dynamical structure. Unresolved and philosophically loaded.

### 2.8 — What happened at (and "before") the Big Bang?
General relativity, extrapolated backward, predicts its own breakdown: a singularity of infinite density where the theory stops making sense. That's not a description of the universe's birth — it's a signpost that we lack the physics (quantum gravity; see physics doc §2.1) to describe it. **Candidate answers:** (a) quantum-gravity resolutions replacing the singularity with a *bounce* (loop quantum cosmology) or a smooth quantum geometry (Hartle–Hawking "no-boundary" proposal, where asking what came "before" is like asking what's north of the North Pole); (b) eternal inflation, in which our Big Bang is one bubble nucleating in an ever-inflating background; (c) cyclic/ekpyrotic models with a prior contracting phase. **Bias flag:** "what caused the Big Bang" is often mis-posed in popular writing as a question about a first moment in time; several serious models dissolve the question rather than answer it. All are currently untested.

### 2.9 — Early structure formation: the "impossible early galaxies"
JWST has found galaxies that appear <cite index="59-1">massive, bright, and morphologically mature only ~300–500 million years after the Big Bang — earlier and more abundant than standard galaxy-formation models predicted</cite>. Early reports framed this as a potential threat to ΛCDM itself. **Candidate answers, and the honest trajectory:** the field is converging — though not unanimously — on *astrophysics, not new cosmology*. Leading explanations include <cite index="62-1">unexpectedly high star-formation efficiency in the early universe, contamination of the samples by active galactic nuclei ("little red dots"), foreground brown dwarfs mimicking high-redshift galaxies, and systematic overestimation of stellar masses</cite>. **Bias flag:** this is a live example of the media-vs-field gap. Headlines said "JWST breaks cosmology"; most specialists read it as "our galaxy-formation models and mass estimates need work." A minority pursue genuinely new physics (early dark energy, exotic dark matter), but treating the anomaly as a confirmed cosmological revolution would misrepresent the field.

### 2.10 — The global shape, uniformity, and fate of the universe
Three linked large-scale questions. **Geometry/topology:** measurements show space is *flat* to within ~0.4%, consistent with an infinite universe — but flat-and-finite (with nontrivial topology, like a 3-torus) is not excluded, and searches for the CMB "matched circles" that would reveal a wrapped-around cosmos continue. **Isotropy anomalies:** the CMB shows statistically odd features — the "Axis of Evil" (an apparent alignment of large-scale multipoles), a hemispherical power asymmetry, and the Cold Spot. **Candidate status:** most likely *look-elsewhere statistical flukes* in a single sky we can't re-sample, but persistently interesting; a minority read them as hints of anisotropic cosmology or pre-inflationary physics. **Fate:** in ΛCDM the universe expands forever toward heat death; but if dark energy is evolving (§2.1) and turns phantom, a *Big Rip* becomes possible, and if Λ can decay, vacuum-decay scenarios loom. The fate is only as knowable as the dark-energy question underneath it.

## 3. Ongoing / Interesting Research Topics (cross-cutting)

- **The dark-sector data deluge.** DESI, Euclid (launched 2023, now returning data), the Vera Rubin Observatory (LSST beginning full operations), and the Roman Space Telescope will over the next several years either confirm evolving dark energy at 5σ+ or dissolve it — this is the single most consequential live thread, and it directly gates §2.1, §2.3, and §2.10's fate question.
- **CMB polarization (B-modes)** remains the cleanest potential window onto inflation (§2.4); next-generation ground experiments (CMB-S4, Simons Observatory) are the ones to watch.
- **Direct and indirect dark-matter searches** continue to *narrow* the WIMP window toward the "neutrino floor," while axion experiments (ADMX and successors) ramp up — the null results are themselves scientifically informative, steadily reshaping which candidate in §2.2 is plausible.
- **The tension between tensions.** A meta-question researchers now take seriously: are the Hubble tension, the S₈/σ₈ (structure-growth) tension, and the dark-energy anomaly *independent* problems or facets of one underlying failure of ΛCDM? Nobody knows yet, and the answer would reshape the field.

## 4. Hard Constraints — Where Fidelity Matters

- **Never present dark matter or dark energy as "discovered substances."** They are terms in an equation standing in for unknown causes. A knowledgeable reader will wince at a character who "understands what dark energy is." You can *invent* an answer for your fiction — that's the point — but the invention should be flagged in-world as a hypothesis or hard-won discovery, not treated as textbook fact.
- **The percentages are not negotiable, and their meaning is often bungled.** ~5% ordinary matter, ~27% dark matter, ~68% dark energy — but these are fractions of the present *energy* budget, and they were different in the past (radiation dominated early; matter dominated the middle epoch; dark energy only recently took over). A story that treats the ratios as eternal constants is wrong.
- **Respect the direction and magnitude of the Hubble tension.** It is real, >5σ, and hardening. Don't hand-wave it as "rounding error"; equally, don't claim it's been resolved — it hasn't.
- **Don't invoke "the Big Bang" as an explosion in space at a point.** It was an expansion *of* space happening everywhere at once. This is the most common cosmological error in fiction, and it's an instant credibility loss with the readership you're courting.
- **Faster-than-light expansion is allowed; faster-than-light travel is not (by the same rule).** Space itself can and does expand such that distant galaxies recede faster than light — general relativity permits this because it's the metric expanding, not objects moving through space. Conflating this into a license for FTL travel is a physics error, though it's a legitimate hook if handled with care (Alcubierre-style metric engineering — see the physics doc's quantum-gravity/exotic-matter material).
- **Distinguish the two "constant" problems.** Whether Λ is constant or evolving (§2.1) is a *different* question from why the vacuum energy isn't 10¹²⁰ times larger (§2.6). Fiction and even journalism routinely blur them.

## 5. Fertile Ground for Creative Extrapolation

- **A civilization that has actually identified the dark sector.** Since "dark matter" and "dark energy" are honest blanks, a story that fills one in — dark matter as a shadow-sector chemistry with its own bound structures, dark energy as an engineerable field — is extrapolating into genuine unknown rather than contradicting known physics. This is among the most legitimate "hard-SF invention" spaces available, precisely because the science is a placeholder.
- **The Hubble tension as a plot premise.** If the two expansion rates genuinely disagree because the universe is more complicated than ΛCDM, a story built around discovering *why* — early dark energy, a varying constant, a local void we sit inside — is riding a real, unresolved frontier.
- **Evolving dark energy → a mutable cosmic fate.** If dark energy strengthens toward phantom behavior, the far future bends from slow heat death toward a Big Rip. A civilization detecting and reacting to its own cosmos's shifting death sentence is a premise grounded in *current* (DESI-era) science, not old textbook certainty.
- **Vacuum decay as an existential, physically-grounded threat.** The cosmological constant problem's ugliness hints our vacuum might be metastable; a bubble of true vacuum expanding at light-speed is a real (if remote) theoretical possibility and an unusually clean doomsday device — no one sees it coming, by construction.
- **CMB anomalies as a buried message or a topological clue.** The Axis of Evil and Cold Spot are *probably* flukes — but "probably" is the writer's friend. A story treating them as a signature of the universe's finite wrapped-around topology, or of a pre-inflationary event, sits right at the edge of what the data can't yet exclude.
- **Inflation's multiverse as setting, not hand-wave.** Eternal inflation is a serious cosmological model that generates other bubble universes with potentially different physics. Used with discipline (respecting that they're causally disconnected and generally unreachable), it's a physically motivated multiverse rather than a lazy one.

## 6. Open Questions

- Is dark energy constant or evolving? (Gated by DESI/Euclid/Rubin within a few years — the field's most answerable big question.)
- What *is* dark matter — particle, and if so which, or a sign that gravity itself needs modifying?
- Is the Hubble tension new physics or an unfound systematic — and is it the same problem as the structure-growth (S₈) tension?
- Did inflation happen, is it eternal, and can the multiverse it implies ever be tested even in principle?
- Why was the early universe's entropy so low — brute fact, or derivable?
- Is the cosmological constant problem solvable without anthropic reasoning, or is the landscape/multiverse the honest answer?
- Are the JWST early-galaxy surprises astrophysics or cosmology? (Leaning astrophysics, not settled.)
- What is the universe's global topology, and are the CMB large-scale anomalies real or flukes?
